### Appendix: Resources and References for Young Thinkers

1. **Key Concepts and Exercises**
   - Confidence Scale: Introduced as a tool to rate claims on a 1–10 scale, inspired by decision-making psychology.
   - Steelmanning: The practice of presenting the strongest version of an opposing argument.

2. **Research and Insights**
   - Calibration of Confidence: Research in cognitive psychology suggesting that rating certainty helps notice hidden assumptions and biases.
   - Argumentation and Debate: Studies indicating that understanding opposing views reduces defensiveness and enhances learning.

3. **Thinking Traps**
   - Argument from Authority: Believing something is true based on the speaker's reputation.
   - Argument from Ignorance: Assuming a claim is true because it hasn’t been proven false.
   - Confirmation Bias: Noticing information that confirms existing beliefs and ignoring opposing evidence.
   - The Bandwagon Effect: Accepting a claim because it is widely believed.
   - False Binary: Presenting only two options when more possibilities exist.

4. **Neuroscience and Critical Thinking**
   - Default Mode Network: Studies show it contributes to resistance to new ideas, emphasizing the importance of revising beliefs.

5. **Engaging Resources**
   - Critical Thinking Websites: Educational platforms like Think Academy offer daily examples and activities for critical thinking.
